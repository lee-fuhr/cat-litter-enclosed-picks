How to choose an enclosed cat litter box

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose an enclosed cat litter box, start by considering your cat’s size, age, and comfort with confinement, then match those needs to a design that balances odor control, easy cleaning, and your home’s space. Prioritize models with removable lids or smooth interior surfaces for maintenance, and always transition slowly rather than swapping boxes overnight.

What are the main types of enclosed litter boxes?

The most common styles are hooded boxes (a standard tray with a lid and swing-open door), dome boxes (rounded with a top opening or small front entry), top-entry boxes (the cat jumps onto the lid and drops in through a hole), and furniture-style boxes (hidden inside a cabinet or planter). Each has trade-offs between privacy, smell containment, and cat acceptance. Hooded boxes are affordable and widely available, but the door can snag or be pushed open by larger cats. Dome boxes often include carbon filters and provide a snug, dark space that many cats prefer. Top-entry boxes excel at preventing scattered litter and blocking view of waste, but require a cat that’s willing to jump and climb. Furniture-style boxes blend into a room but can be harder to fully clean because of the outer enclosure.

How do I choose the right size for my cat?

An enclosed box should be roomy enough for your cat to turn around, squat, and dig without touching the walls or hood. For a typical adult cat, look for a model that’s at least one and a half times the cat’s nose-to-tail length inside. Large breeds or long-bodied cats may need a jumbo or an open-topped extra-large model. Height matters too: a low ceiling inside a dome can make a tall cat feel cramped. Measure your cat’s height when sitting and ensure the interior clearance is a few inches more. If the entrance is too small or the step-in height is too high, senior or arthritic cats will struggle. In general, err on the larger side; most cats dislike feeling trapped in a tight space.

What should I look for in odor control and ventilation?

Enclosed boxes trap smells inside, which can be a blessing and a curse. Without some airflow, the interior becomes a concentrated odor zone that might drive your cat away. Look for models with a charcoal filter in the top or a small vent that allows gas exchange while still containing litter dust. Some domes have a snap-in filter panel that can be replaced monthly. Be realistic: no enclosed box eliminates daily scooping or weekly deep cleans. Filters reduce airborne particles but can’t mask ammonia buildup from neglected waste. If you have multiple cats, you may need a larger box with extra ventilation or a top-entry design that lets odors rise into a carbon pad. Avoid boxes with fully sealed lids that lack any vent – those create a stale, high-humidity environment that encourages bacterial growth.

How important is ease of cleaning and maintenance?

This is often the make-or-break feature. A box that’s hard to clean will be cleaned less often, which leads to odor problems and unhappy cats. Look for models where the entire top lifts off without tools, the pan has smooth rounded corners (no crevices for urine to pool), and the entrance door can be removed or propped open during scrubbing. Some top-entry boxes have a two-piece lid that separates fully; dome boxes typically come in two halves that snap apart. Avoid designs where you have to reach through a small opening to scrape the sides. Non-stick interior coatings help, but they wear over time – consider lining the bottom with a reusable liner that can be hosed down quickly. If you can’t get a scrub brush into every corner, move on to a simpler design.

Which entry style works best for different cats?

Front-entry boxes with a small swinging door work for most cats, especially if the door is clear or lightly tinted so the cat can see out. Some cats, though, refuse to push through a flimsy plastic door. A doorless front opening (simply a cutout) is more inviting but lets out more scent and litter. Top-entry boxes are great for non-jumpers? – not usually. They require a spring upward of about 8 to 12 inches, which can be daunting for kittens, seniors, or cats with mobility issues. For those cats, a low-sided dome with a wide front entry is better. Step-in thresholds (a slight ramp or low edge) help arthritic cats and also reduce litter tracking. Observe your cat’s current box behavior: do they back out, dig excessively, or hesitate? That will guide whether they need a fully open top or a generous front door.

What materials and construction should I prioritize?

Good plastic density matters. Thin, flimsy plastic cracks over time, especially around screw holes or clip points, and it absorbs odor faster. Look for models made from polypropylene or a similar food-grade plastic that resists staining. The interior surface should be non-porous; matte finishes are acceptable but can trap hair and dander more than glossy ones. Pay attention to hinge and clip quality. Many budget boxes use small plastic tabs that break after a few lid removals. Heavier-duty units often have metal-reinforced hinges or sliding locks. If the box will sit on carpet, check if the base has rubber feet or a non-skid pad to prevent shifting. Finally, consider weight: a very heavy dome is stable but hard to lift during cleaning; a lighter one is easier to manage but may slide when the cat jumps out.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use an enclosed box for multiple cats?

Yes, but you’ll need one box per cat plus an extra, and each box should be oversized to avoid territorial disputes. Some enclosed designs with small doors only allow one cat at a time, which can cause bullying. A dome or top-entry box with a wider interior may work, but plan for extra cleaning frequency.

Will my cat be scared of an enclosed box?

Many cats are initially wary because the hood blocks peripheral vision and amplifies sounds. Start by removing the lid or door for a week, then reattach gradually. Place the box in a quiet, low-traffic area and never force your cat inside. A confident cat usually adapts within two weeks.

How often should I clean an enclosed litter box?

Scoop solid waste at least daily – more often if you have multiple cats. Deep-clean the entire box (dump all litter, wash with mild detergent, rinse, dry) every two to four weeks. Replace carbon filters according to the manufacturer’s recommendation, typically monthly.

Are top-entry boxes better for odor control?

They can be because waste drops into a deep, covered basin and airborne odors tend to rise into a filter tray on the lid. However, they still need regular scooping. The real advantage is that litter stays inside better, reducing scattered waste that can smell elsewhere.

What’s the best type for senior or disabled cats?

A low-profile dome with a wide, doorless entry and no step-up threshold is ideal. Avoid top-entry boxes that require jumping. Look for a box with a removable lid so you can convert it to a low-sided pan if needed. Also consider a box with a ramp or a flat entrance at floor level.

Do enclosed boxes really trap more dust?

Yes, and that can be a problem for cats with respiratory issues or for owners with allergies. Some models have vents or filter systems that reduce airborne dust, but no enclosure is airtight. Using a low-dust, unscented litter and ventilating the room helps. Opening the top during cleaning also minimizes inhalation.

In shortThe best enclosed litter box is the one your cat actually uses consistently, so watch their behavior closely during the transition. Combine a design that’s easy for you to clean with one that respects your cat’s need for privacy and escape routes. If you prioritize solid construction, smart venting, and a simple cleaning process, you’ll find a box that keeps odors low and both you and your cat content.
