Enclosed cat litter box questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

An enclosed cat litter box is any box with a cover, hood, dome, or top entry that provides privacy and helps contain mess and odor. The best choice depends on your cat’s size, personality, and your cleaning habits, not on a single “best” type.

What is an enclosed cat litter box?

An enclosed cat litter box is a litter box that has some form of cover: a hood that snaps onto a standard pan, a dome-shaped shell, or a box with a hole in the top that cats jump into. The main goal is to give cats privacy while keeping litter, dust, and odors more contained than an open pan. These boxes come in several styles. Hooded boxes are the most common, a plastic cover arches over a tray, often with a swinging door. Dome boxes are round or oval with a single opening. Top-entry boxes have a lid with a hole; the cat jumps onto the lid and drops down into the box. Each style has trade-offs in cleaning ease, cat acceptance, and space requirements.

Hooded vs dome vs top-entry: which is best?

There is no single best style. Hooded boxes are widely available and budget-friendly, but the door can be annoying for some cats and they can trap odors if not cleaned often. Dome boxes offer a spacious interior and good air circulation, but they take up more floor space. Top-entry boxes are excellent for stopping scatter and for homes with dogs, because paws can’t reach in. However, they require the cat to jump, which may be hard for seniors or kittens. Your choice should prioritize your cat’s comfort. Most cats prefer a box that doesn’t feel cramped and has at least one clear escape route. A hooded box with the door removed is often safer for hesitant cats. A top-entry box works best for agile, confident jumpers.

Will my cat use an enclosed box?

Many cats accept enclosed boxes easily, but some do not. Cats that are naturally cautious or have had a bad experience with a stuck door may refuse a covered box. A common solution is to remove the swinging door (most hooded boxes allow this) so the cat can see out and feel less trapped. Top-entry boxes can be especially intimidating for cats that dislike being confined or have trouble balancing. If your cat starts avoiding the box, try offering both an open pan and an enclosed box side by side for a week. Watch which one gets used. Also, ensure the box is large enough; a small hooded box can feel cramped for a big cat. Brands like Catit, Modkat, and Frisco make roomier versions that many cats accept.

How do I clean and maintain an enclosed box?

Cleaning an enclosed box is slightly more involved than an open pan because you have to remove the hood or lid. For hooded boxes, lift off the cover and scoop daily. The hood itself collects dust and urine spray, so wipe it down with a mild detergent or enzymatic cleaner weekly. Dome boxes typically have a separate top and bottom that snap apart, handy for deep cleaning. Top-entry boxes require lifting the lid; some have a rim that traps litter, so you’ll want to sweep the top before and after scooping. No matter the style, avoid scented cleaners or strong chemicals because cats are sensitive to smells. A 10% bleach solution (rinse thoroughly) or a pet-safe enzyme spray works well. Replace the entire box every year or two, since plastic absorbs odors over time.

Do enclosed boxes really control odor?

An enclosed box can reduce the spread of odor through a room, but it does not eliminate smell. The cover traps some odorous air inside, which can actually make the box smellier for the cat. Good ventilation (especially in dome designs with a top vent) helps, but the only real solution is frequent scooping, at least once a day, and a full litter change every two to four weeks. Activated charcoal filters are built into many hooded and dome boxes (e.g., the Breeze Hooded Box or the Nature’s Miracle hooded models). These filters help absorb some airborne odors, but they are not a substitute for cleaning. If you smell the box from across the room, it is time to scoop or change the litter entirely.

What size should I choose?

Size matters greatly. A box that is too small will make your cat feel cramped and may lead to accidents outside the box. Most enclosed boxes are designed for average cats (8–12 pounds). Large cats, long-bodied cats, or cats that like to sprawl need an extra-large model. Look for boxes that are at least 1.5 times the length of your cat from nose to tail base. Top-entry boxes often have generous floor space but lower headroom, some cats dislike that. Dome boxes are usually the tallest, giving a cat room to stand and turn. Hooded boxes vary widely; check the dimensions and read owner reviews about space. Brands like IRIS and Modkat offer large options that many cat owners recommend for bigger breeds.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a regular plastic litter liner in a top-entry box?

Yes, but it may bunch or tear more easily because the cat jumps onto the lid and then claws the liner. Many owners skip liners in top-entry boxes and rely on easy-clean plastic that can be wiped down.

Do enclosed boxes trap smells inside and make the box worse?

They can concentrate odor inside, which some cats may find off-putting. Choosing a box with a charcoal filter or good ventilation (e.g., a dome with a top vent) helps, but daily scooping is the real answer.

Are top-entry litter boxes good for senior cats?

They are generally not recommended for seniors or cats with mobility issues because jumping up and down can be painful or impossible. A hooded box with a low entrance or a large open pan is better for older cats.

How often should I scoop an enclosed box?

At least once daily. The enclosed space holds smells tighter, so skipping a day makes the box unpleasant for both you and your cat. Twice daily is ideal if you can manage it.

Can I put a hooded litter box in a corner?

Yes, but beware that cleaning may become more awkward if you can’t easily reach the sides. Also, some cats dislike being backed into a corner; leaving a few inches of space on one side can help them feel less trapped.

Do dome litter boxes have enough ventilation?

Most dome boxes include a top vent or small side holes to let air circulate. This reduces condensation and odor buildup. If the dome has no vents, you can safely drill a few small holes, just be careful not to crack the plastic.

In shortThe best enclosed box is one your cat actually uses and that you can keep clean without a hassle. Start by matching the style to your cat’s personality, if they spook easily, skip the door. Prioritize size over fancy features, and never rely on filters or hoods to replace daily scooping. A little trial and error with a budget-friendly model is smarter than investing in an expensive box your cat ignores.
